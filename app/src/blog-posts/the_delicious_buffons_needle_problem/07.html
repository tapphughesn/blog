Is this method of estimating pi any good? No, not really--much better methods of estimating pi exist. The expected value of this estimator is exactly pi, but its variance (the calculation of which I’ll leave as an exercise for the reader) is quite high, leaving much to be desired. Never mind the fact that we can calculate digits of pi directly.

We can improve this estimator, however, by incorporating a little bit of a priori knowledge. It is well known that 355/113 is a good approximation of pi (accurate to six decimal places). For a bit of fun, try setting the number of needles to 355, and see if you can get 113 crossings. If you were open to a bit of deceit, you could use this experimental result to exaggerate the efficacy of this method of estimating pi. Surely no one would do such a thing!

Historical Hogwash

Academic fraud, despite being ever more relevant, is not a problem of the mere present. In the early 20th century, the Italian mathematician Mario Lazzarini claimed to have physically set up the Buffon’s Needle Problem experiment with l = 2.5 cm, d = 3 cm, and the number of needle droppings = 3408. He counted 1808 crossings, thus giving the approximation:

pi \approx \frac{2l(\text{\# needles})}{d(\text{\# crossings})} = \frac{2 \times 2.5 \times 3408}{3 \times 1808} = \frac{355}{113} = 3.1415929...

Lazzarini then published a paper with this result, triumphantly showing the efficacy of this method. He had forged the experiment. He deliberately chose a number of needle droppings that would produce the 355/113 approximation. Furthermore, he doesn’t provide any estimates of measurement error for the length of l and d, which is relevant since a tiny physical measurement error could reduce the accuracy of the pi estimation by multiple decimal places. Professor Lee Badger’s excellent Mathematics Magazine article “Lazzarini’s Lucky Approximation of pi” goes into more detail about Lazzarini’s hoax.
