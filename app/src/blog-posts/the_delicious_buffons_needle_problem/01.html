The Delicious Buffon’s Needle Problem

There are some math problems that, either because of their elegance, their history, or their use as a tool for learning, stand out above the rest. Buffon’s needle problem is one that does all three. It is an absolute gem that combines basic geometry with some undergraduate-level probability theory. Although the problem is relatively easy to understand, the resulting discussion will involve puzzlement, surprise, elegance, and a touch of academic fraud. I envy those who have not yet come across this problem and I hope that there is still some enjoyment here for those who have1.

The Problem

Let’s consider this wonderful conundrum as it was originally posed in 1777 by Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon:

Suppose we have a floor made of parallel strips of wood, each the same width, and we drop a needle onto the floor. What is the probability that the needle will lie across a line between two strips?

To further specify the question, let’s say that the width of the strips of wood is d, the length of the needle is l, and the length of the needle is not greater than the width of the strips of wood, i.e. l ≤ d. This is the “short needle” case (solving the long needle case is slightly more complicated). The picture looks something like this:



At this point, you might be starting to feel out some potential approaches you could take. You might ask some questions like “Does the floor have a boundary, or is it an infinite plane?” (it’s an infinite plane), “Is the position of each needle independent from the other needles?” (yes), and “Are we assuming that the random position and orientation of the needle are uniformly distributed?” (yes).
