The Proof

First let’s observe that, if we fix d, the number of crossings depends entirely on l--there are no other variables that influence the probability of a crossing. Let E(l) be the number of crossings we expect to get from dropping one needle of length l. We want to find the formula for E(l), expressed in terms of l and the (fixed) d.

Polygonal Needles

In the last section, we considered two needles that were arranged in a line, head-to-tail, and stated that the expected number of crossings of this couple of needles was equal to the sum of the expected number of crossings of each of its constituent needles. This argument holds for any arrangement of the constituent needles--it doesn’t matter how the positions of needles are dependent upon one another. So, these two needles both have the same expected number of crossings:

[Image: long needle and angled needle]

Both of these needles have an expected number of crossings 2E(l) = E(2l) = 2cl.

The same argument holds for a complex of needles consisting of more than two segments. In particular, a polygonal needle made of n segments of length l will have an expected number of crossings cnl.
